# Chapter 3 — ICU Clinical Workflow

Understanding the nurse's workflow is essential to designing an effective alert triage system. Every intervention must fit into existing rhythms without adding friction.

## 3.1 Patient Admission

A patient arrives from the ED or OR. The ICU team stabilizes, connects monitors, and establishes baseline vitals. The nurse performs an initial head-to-toe assessment and documents in the EHR.

## 3.2 Initial Assessment

Within the first hour, the nurse:
- Reviews the patient's history and reason for admission
- Sets alarm thresholds based on the patient's condition
- Confirms all monitoring equipment is functioning
- Establishes baseline vital signs
- Documents the plan of care

## 3.3 Bed Allocation

The charge nurse assigns patients to nurses based on acuity, nurse experience, and bed availability. High-acuity patients (e.g., post-cardiac arrest, septic shock) get 1:1 nursing.

## 3.4 Monitoring Begins

Continuous vitals monitoring starts immediately. The bedside monitor displays heart rate, blood pressure, oxygen saturation, respiratory rate, and temperature. Alarms trigger when values fall outside configured thresholds.

## 3.5 Continuous Observation

The nurse's shift consists of:
- **Routine checks** — Hourly vitals documentation, medication administration, repositioning
- **Alarm response** — Interrupt-driven; nurse must assess, decide, and act
- **Ongoing assessment** — Trend monitoring, subtle changes that may not trigger alarms

This is where alert fatigue has its greatest impact. Each alarm interrupts the nurse's current task, requiring context switching that increases cognitive load and error risk.

## 3.6 Shift Handover

At shift change (typically every 12 hours), the outgoing nurse briefs the incoming nurse on:
- Patient status and stability
- Active alerts and recent events
- Pending tasks and medications
- Changes in condition or treatment plan

Handover is high-risk for information loss. An alert timeline and history — features of CarePulse AI — can significantly improve handover quality.

## 3.7 Documentation Workflow

Nurses spend approximately 25-35% of their time on documentation. Key documentation points:
- Vital signs (hourly or more frequently)
- Alert responses (what was the alarm, what action was taken)
- Medication administration
- Assessment findings
- Communication with physicians

## 3.8 Patient Discharge

When the patient is stable enough for a general ward:
- Monitoring equipment is removed
- Discharge summary is prepared
- Handover to ward nurse is completed
- Bed is cleaned and prepared for next admission

## Research Finding

The alarm response workflow is highly interrupt-driven. Nurses cannot plan their time around alarms because alarms are unpredictable, numerous, and mostly irrelevant. This unpredictability is a primary driver of cognitive fatigue.

## Product Opportunity

An intelligent triage system that filters irrelevant alarms and ranks remaining alerts by urgency would allow nurses to:
- Trust that visible alerts are meaningful
- Plan their response sequence (most critical patient first)
- Reduce context switching by batching non-urgent alerts
